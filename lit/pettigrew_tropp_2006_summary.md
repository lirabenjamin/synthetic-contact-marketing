# Literature Summary: Pettigrew & Tropp (2006)

## Citation
Pettigrew, T. F., & Tropp, L. R. (2006). A meta-analytic test of intergroup contact theory. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 90*(5), 751-783.

## Overview
This comprehensive meta-analysis represents the most extensive test of intergroup contact theory to date, analyzing 713 independent samples from 515 studies spanning 1940-2000, with over 250,000 participants from 38 nations. The study provides definitive evidence that intergroup contact typically reduces prejudice and examines the conditions under which contact is most effective.

---

## Research Questions

1. **Does intergroup contact reduce prejudice?**
2. **Are Allport's (1954) optimal contact conditions necessary for prejudice reduction?**
3. **Do contact effects generalize beyond immediate participants?**
4. **Can alternative explanations (selection bias, publication bias) account for contact-prejudice effects?**

---

## Historical Context

### Early Contact Theory Development

**Williams (1947)** provided the first systematic formulation, proposing that intergroup contact maximally reduces prejudice when groups share:
- Similar status
- Similar interests and tasks
- Personal, intimate interaction

**Allport (1954)** offered the most influential statement, identifying four optimal conditions:
1. **Equal status** between groups in the situation
2. **Common goals**
3. **Intergroup cooperation**
4. **Support of authorities, law, or custom**

### Early Empirical Support

- **Brophy (1946)**: White Merchant Marine seamen became more positive toward Blacks with more integrated voyages
- **Kephart (1957)**: White police officers working with Black colleagues showed reduced objections to integration
- **Deutsch & Collins (1951)**: Classic housing study showing White residents in integrated projects held more positive attitudes toward Black neighbors

## Method

### Inclusion Criteria

**Criterion 1**: Studies must examine contact as independent variable and prejudice as dependent variable

**Criterion 2**: Research must involve contact between members of **discrete groups** (not just interpersonal contact)

**Criterion 3**: Must involve **actual face-to-face interaction** - not just proximity or opportunity for contact. This excluded:
- Studies using group proportions to infer contact
- Research assuming contact from proximity without verification
- Studies measuring attitudes toward contact without actual contact experience

**Criterion 4**: Individual-level analysis (not just aggregate outcomes)

### Study Characteristics

**Sample Composition**:
- 515 studies yielding 713 independent samples
- 1,383 individual tests
- 250,089 total participants
- Median publication year: 1986
- 38 countries represented (71% from United States)

**Study Types**:
- Survey/field research: 71%
- Quasi-experiments: 24%
- True experiments: 5%

**Target Groups**:
- Racial/ethnic groups: 51% of samples
- Other targets: Sexual orientation, physically disabled, mentally disabled, mentally ill, elderly

### Analytical Approach

**Model Selection**: Random effects model (rather than fixed effects) because:
- Studies are highly heterogeneous
- Treatments are ill-specified across studies
- Effects are complex and multidetermined
- Allows generalization beyond included studies

**Effect Size**: Pearson's r (negative values = contact reduces prejudice)

**Conservative Procedures**:
- Studies reporting only "nonsignificant" results assigned r = .00
- Capped extremely large samples (5,000 for studies, 3,000 for samples, 2,000 for tests)
- Multiple tests for publication bias and selection effects

---

## Key Findings

### Overall Contact-Prejudice Relationship

**Primary Result**: Strong inverse relationship between contact and prejudice
- **Mean r = -.21** (random effects, with corrections)
- **94% of samples** showed inverse relationship
- Effect holds across all units of analysis (studies, samples, tests)
- Highly significant (p < .0001) despite conservative random effects model

**Interpretation**: Though "small to medium" by Cohen's (1988) standards, this represents:
- A highly significant and replicable effect
- Consistency across diverse contexts, methods, and groups
- Substantially larger effects in rigorous studies

### Threat to Validity Tests

#### 1. Causal Sequence Problem (Selection Bias)

**Concern**: Do tolerant people seek contact rather than contact reducing prejudice?

**Test**: Compared samples by degree of participant choice in engaging contact
- **No choice samples**: Mean r = -.280
- **Some choice**: Mean r = -.190  
- **Full choice**: Mean r = -.218

**Finding**: Samples allowing NO choice (eliminating selection bias) showed **larger** effects than those allowing choice (QB(1) = 20.58, p < .0001). When controlling for research quality, choice was unrelated to effect size (r = .005, p = .89).

**Conclusion**: Selection bias does not explain contact effects; if anything, it **underestimates** true causal impact.

#### 2. Publication Bias

**Multiple Tests Conducted**:

**a) Fail-safe N**: Would require 1,200+ unpublished null studies to erase significance (far more than the 713 samples found in intensive 6-year search)

**b) Sample size × Effect size correlation**: 
- Not significant (r = -.02 for all samples, r = -.04 for analysis samples)
- No relationship suggests limited publication bias

**c) Funnel plot**: 
- Roughly symmetrical distribution
- Similar mean (r = -.216), median (r = -.205), and mode (r = -.210)
- Suggests minimal bias

**d) Trim-and-fill method** (Duval & Tweedie): 
- Estimated ~72 (10.3%) missing samples
- Adjusted effect size Z = -.245 (similar to unadjusted)

**e) Published vs. Unpublished comparison**:
- Published sources: Mean r = -.211
- Unpublished sources: Mean r = -.237
- Difference not significant (QB(1) = 2.17, p = .14)
- **88 unpublished studies** located (70% dissertations)

**f) General linear model approach** (Vevea & Hedges):
- Most conservative test
- Found no significant overall effect when adjusting for missing small studies
- **However**: Still found significant effect (Z = -.109, p = .02) for rigorous studies with strong controls

**Conclusion**: 5 of 6 tests suggest publication bias is not a major threat. The one exception still found effects in most rigorous research.

#### 3. Generalization of Effects

**Critical Question**: Do effects extend beyond immediate contact participants?

**Four Types of Generalization Tested** (using 1,365 tests):

1. **Within situation** (to other outgroup members present): Mean r = -.231 (k = 152)
2. **Across situations** (same outgroup, different settings): Mean r = -.244 (k = 17)
3. **To whole outgroup** (primary focus of most studies): Mean r = -.213 (k = 1,164)
4. **To other outgroups** (not involved in contact): Mean r = -.190 (k = 18)

**Key Finding**: No significant differences between generalization types (QB(3) = 1.61, ns). Effects generalize **broadly**—even to outgroups not involved in the contact.

**Implication**: Contact effects are **not** limited to immediate participants, addressing a major criticism of contact theory. Generalization appears far broader than commonly assumed.

#### 4. Research Rigor

**Five Indicators of Quality Tested**:

**a) Type of Study**:
- True experiments: Mean r = -.336 (largest effect)
- Quasi-experiments: Mean r = -.237
- Surveys/field studies: Mean r = -.204
- Differences significant (QB(2) = 18.51, p < .001)

**Interpretation**: Experiments (with random assignment, eliminating selection) show **strongest** effects, approaching Cohen's "large" effect size (d = .713).

**b) Quality of Contact Measure**:
- Observed contact: Mean r = -.246
- Self-reported contact: Mean r = -.210
- Assumed contact: Mean r = -.132
- Differences significant (QB(2) = 16.44, p < .001)

**c) Quality of Prejudice Measure**:
- Multi-item, high reliability (α ≥ .70): Mean r = -.246
- Other reliable measures: Mean r = -.293
- Multi-item, low/unknown reliability: Mean r = -.190
- Single item: Mean r = -.233
- Differences significant (QB(3) = 20.86, p < .001)

**d) Type of Control Group** (for between-subjects designs):
- No prior outgroup contact: Mean r = -.244
- Some prior contact: Mean r = -.209
- Extensive prior contact: Mean r = -.138
- Differences significant (QB(2) = 15.78, p < .001)

**e) Quality of Both Measures Combined**:
- **Highest rigor subset** (n = 77): Mean r = -.323
- Remaining samples: Mean r = -.202
- Difference highly significant (QB(1) = 35.96, p < .0001)

**Critical Finding**: More rigorous research consistently yields **larger** effect sizes. This is opposite to what would be expected if effects were artifacts. Less rigorous studies **reduce** observed effects.

## Allport's Optimal Conditions

### Global Test: Structured Programs

**Comparison**:
- Structured programs (designed to meet Allport's conditions): Mean r = -.287 (k = 134)
- Other samples: Mean r = -.204 (k = 562)
- Difference significant (QB(1) = 20.19, p < .0001)

**Key Finding**: Optimal conditions **enhance** but are **not essential** for positive effects. Even without structured conditions, 562 samples still showed significant prejudice reduction (mean r = -.204).

### Multivariate Test

**Regression Model** controlling for methodological rigor:
- Study type
- Contact measure quality
- Prejudice measure quality  
- Control group adequacy

**Result**: Structured programs remained significant predictor (β = .099, p = .03) even controlling for research quality.

**Interpretation**: Allport's conditions provide genuine enhancement beyond measurement artifacts.

### Individual Conditions Analysis

**Subset**: 134 samples with structured programs
**Conditions Rated**: Common goals, cooperation, equal status, authority sanction

**Key Findings**:

1. **No individual condition** showed significant independent effects
2. Conditions highly intercorrelated (rs = .51 to .63)
3. **72% of samples** had at least 3 of 4 conditions
4. None emerged as significant predictor in regression models

**Authority Sanction Special Analysis**:
- Authority sanction only (k = 31): Mean r = -.286
- Two or more conditions (k = 103): Mean r = -.290  
- No significant difference (QB(1) = 0.01, p = .93)
- **Both significantly stronger** than remaining samples (r = -.204)

**Interpretation**: Allport's conditions work as an **interrelated bundle** rather than independent factors. Authority sanction may be particularly important, but conditions should be implemented together.

### Cross-Group Friendship

**Secondary test** of optimal conditions (friendship involves cooperation, common goals, equal status, repeated contact):
- Friendship measure (k = 154 tests): Mean r = -.246
- Other contact measures: Mean r = -.212
- Difference significant (QB(1) = 4.42, p < .05)

## Moderator Analyses

### Target Group Differences

**Largest Effects**:
- **Gay men and lesbians**: Mean r = -.271 (significantly larger than others, QB(1) = 5.34, p = .02)
- **Physically disabled**: Mean r = -.243 (larger than average)

**Average Effects**:
- **Racial/ethnic groups**: Mean r = -.214 (most studied)
- **Mentally disabled**: Mean r = -.202

**Smaller Effects**:
- **Mentally ill and elderly** (combined): Mean r = -.183 (significantly lower, QB(1) = 4.51, p = .03)

**Interpretation**: Contact theory applies across diverse target groups, though effectiveness varies. Effects for racial/ethnic groups (theory's original focus) are neither largest nor smallest.

### Participant Age

- **Children** (1-12 years): Mean r = -.239
- **College students**: Mean r = -.231
- **Adolescents**: Mean r = -.208
- **Adults**: Mean r = -.197

**Key Comparisons**:
- Children vs. adults: Marginally stronger (QB(1) = 3.59, p = .06)
- College students vs. adults: **Significantly** stronger (QB(1) = 5.49, p = .05)
- No other differences significant

**Interpretation**: Consistent with Sears (1986), college students show greater openness to change. Contact may be more effective with younger, more malleable attitudes.

### Participant Sex

- All-female samples: Mean r = -.214
- All-male samples: Mean r = -.185
- Mixed/undetermined: Mean r = -.218
- **No significant differences** (QB(2) = 1.83, ns)

**Interpretation**: Sex is not an important moderator of contact effects.

### Geographic Area

**Six Regions Tested**:
- United States (71% of samples): Mean r = -.215
- Europe: Mean r = -.217
- Israel: Mean r = -.196
- Canada: Mean r = -.232
- Australia/New Zealand: Mean r = -.259
- Africa/Asia/Latin America: Mean r = -.205

**Key Finding**: No significant differences across regions (QB(5) = 1.88, p = .87). U.S. vs. non-U.S. samples virtually identical (QB(1) = 0.01, p = .90).

**Interpretation**: Contact effects are **universal**, not culture-specific. Theory developed in American context generalizes globally.

### Contact Setting

**Largest Effects**:
- **Laboratory**: Mean r = -.273
- **Recreational**: Mean r = -.299
- Combined recreational/laboratory vs. others: QB(1) = 6.86, p = .01

**Average Effects**:
- Work/organizational: Mean r = -.224
- Educational: Mean r = -.213
- Mixed/other: Mean r = -.213
- Residential: Mean r = -.202

**Smallest Effects**:
- **Tourism/travel**: Mean r = -.113 (significantly smaller, QB(1) = 3.84, p = .05)

**Interpretation**: Settings vary in contact intensity/duration. Controlled, cooperative settings (lab, recreation) show strongest effects. Brief tourist contact shows weakest effects.

### Date of Publication

**Temporal Trend**:
- Prior to 1960: Mean r = -.228
- 1960-1969: Mean r = -.176
- 1970-1979: Mean r = -.169
- **1980-1989**: Mean r = -.233
- **1990-2000**: Mean r = -.238
- Post-1979 vs. pre-1980: QB(1) = 15.59, p < .0001

**Key Finding**: Recent research (post-1979) shows **significantly larger** effects than earlier work.

**Explanation**: Largely due to increased research rigor:
- Better contact measures (χ²(1) = 13.70, p < .001)
- Better prejudice measures (χ²(1) = 52.62, p < .001)  
- Better control groups (χ²(2) = 12.14, p < .01)

After controlling for quality, temporal difference reduced but remained significant (β = .08, p = .05).

**Interpretation**: Modern research is more rigorous. Contact effects may also genuinely strengthen over time as societies become more egalitarian.

## Subset Analyses: Racial/Ethnic vs. Other Groups

### Comparison of Mean Effects

- **Racial/ethnic samples** (k = 362): Mean r = -.218
- **Other samples** (k = 334): Mean r = -.220
- **No significant difference** (QB(1) = 0.027, p = .87)

**Critical Implication**: Contact theory, originally developed for racial/ethnic prejudice, **generalizes to other target groups** with equal effectiveness.

### Methodological Moderators by Subset

**Similar Patterns for Both Subsets**:
- Higher quality contact and prejudice measures → larger effects
- Better controls → larger effects
- Experiments → larger effects (especially for non-racial/ethnic)

**Some Variations**:
- Study type more important for non-racial/ethnic samples
- Control group quality more important for non-racial/ethnic samples
- Prejudice measure quality more important for racial/ethnic samples

### Allport's Conditions by Subset

**Racial/Ethnic Samples**:
- Structured programs: Mean r = -.262
- No program: Mean r = -.210
- Difference: QB(1) = 2.62, p = .11 (marginal)

**Non-Racial/Ethnic Samples**:
- Structured programs: Mean r = -.299
- No program: Mean r = -.194
- Difference: QB(1) = 19.67, p < .001 (highly significant)

**Interpretation**: Optimal conditions appear **more important** for non-racial/ethnic contact (disabilities, mental illness, elderly). This may reflect:
- Less societal support for contact with these groups
- More anxiety/uncertainty in these interactions
- Greater need for structure to reduce threat

### Multivariate Models by Subset

**Both Models** (Table 10):
- Structured programs: Marginally significant predictor in both (β = .112, p = .069 for racial/ethnic; β = .105, p = .094 for others)
- Quality of measures: Significant predictors in both
- Similar overall R² values

**Conclusion**: Despite minor differences, both subsets show **remarkably similar patterns**. Contact theory applies across intergroup contexts.

## Theoretical Implications

### Why Contact Works: Moving Beyond Allport

The authors propose that contact reduces prejudice through a more fundamental mechanism than Allport specified: **Familiarity breeds liking** (Zajonc, 1968; Homans, 1950).

**Mere Exposure Perspective**:
1. Greater exposure to targets enhances liking (Zajonc, 1968; Bornstein, 1989)
2. This effect generalizes to related, unknown targets (Rhodes et al., 2001)
3. Exposure effects occur even without Allport's optimal conditions

**Supporting Evidence from Meta-Analysis**:
- 94% of samples show contact reduces prejudice
- Only 19% had structured programs with optimal conditions
- Effects persist even in suboptimal situations (apartheid South Africa, segregated neighborhoods)

**Examples of Contact Without Optimal Conditions**:
- **Van Dyk (1990)**: White South African housewives with African domestic workers showed less prejudice (r = .09) despite:
  - Apartheid context
  - Severe status inequality
  - Lack of authority support
  - No cooperation or common goals

- **Crain & Weisman (1972)**: African Americans who played with Whites as children were less anti-White (r = .08) despite:
  - Segregated schools and neighborhoods
  - No institutional support
  - Informal, unsupervised contact

### The Role of Uncertainty and Anxiety Reduction

**Emerging Theoretical Integration**:
- Uncertainty reduction (Lee, 2001) underlies familiarity→liking
- Intergroup anxiety (Stephan & Stephan, 1985) mediates contact→prejudice
- Anxiety reflects threat + uncertainty in intergroup interactions

**Supporting Research**:
- Contact reduces threat and anxiety (Blair et al., 2003; Islam & Hewstone, 1993; Paolini et al., 2004)
- Anxiety mediates contact-prejudice relationship (Paolini et al., 2004; Voci & Hewstone, 2003)
- Effects strongest when anxiety is reduced

**Allport's Conditions Reconsidered**:
- Optimal conditions **enhance** positive effects by reducing anxiety and threat
- They are **facilitating conditions**, not necessary preconditions
- They work as an **interrelated bundle** (not independent factors)
- **Authority support** may be particularly important for legitimizing contact and reducing threat

### What Prevents Contact from Working?

**New Research Direction**: Focus should shift from positive facilitators to **negative inhibitors**:

**Negative Factors Identified**:
- Intergroup anxiety (Stephan & Stephan, 1985)
- Threat perceptions (Stephan et al., 2002)
- Authoritarianism (Pettigrew et al., 2006)
- Normative restraints
- Negative intergroup experiences (Plant, 2004; Plant & Devine, 2003)
- Lack of institutional support

**Historical Context**: Early theorists (Williams, 1947; Allport, 1954) assumed most contact failed to reduce prejudice, so they identified positive conditions. Meta-analysis reveals most contact actually **does** reduce prejudice, making negative inhibitors more theoretically important.

### Group Salience as Moderator

**Recent Findings** (Voci & Hewstone, 2003):
- Anxiety mediates contact→prejudice when group salience is **high**
- Less mediation when salience is **low**
- Effects more likely to generalize when group membership is **salient** (Brown & Hewstone, 2005)

**Implication for Meta-Analysis**: 
- Contact research situations likely made group membership salient
- This may explain widespread generalization observed
- Future research should systematically vary salience

## Expanded Contact Effects

Beyond prejudice reduction, recent research examines contact's effects on:

**Cognitive Outcomes**:
- Intergroup differentiation (Islam & Hewstone, 1993)
- Outgroup variability perceptions (Oaker & Brown, 1986; Paolini et al., 2004)
- Broadened ingroup views (Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000)

**Affective Outcomes**:
- Ingroup pride (London & Linney, 1993)
- Empathy and perspective-taking (Craig et al., 2002)
- Trust and forgiveness (Hewstone et al., 2005)

**Future Directions**: Comprehensive models should integrate:
- Multiple mediators (anxiety, empathy, perspective-taking, expanded ingroup views)
- Positive and negative factors
- Group salience effects
- Individual differences
- Structural and normative antecedents

## Limitations and Future Research Needs

### Methodological Limitations

1. **Cross-Sectional Dominance**: Most studies are cross-sectional
   - Cannot definitively establish causality
   - Selection bias tests are indirect
   - Need more longitudinal research

2. **Information Quality About Conditions**: 
   - Only 134 samples provided enough detail to rate individual conditions
   - Could not reliably assess Allport's conditions for most studies
   - Future research should systematically document contact characteristics

3. **Incomplete Coverage**: 
   - Despite intensive search, file likely incomplete
   - Some unpublished studies remain undiscovered
   - Publication bias tests yield mixed results

### Critical Research Gaps

**1. Longitudinal Studies**
- Few studies track effects over time (Eller & Abrams, 2003; Levin et al., 2003)
- Unknown: How long do effects persist?
- Unknown: Does prejudice reduction asymptote with continued contact?
- Prediction: Similar to mere exposure, effects will plateau

**2. Negative Contact**
- Most studies focus on positive contact
- Recent work shows negative experiences increase anxiety and threat (Plant, 2004)
- Need systematic study of when contact backfires

**3. Comprehensive Models**
**Current State**: Multiple partial models emerging (Brown & Hewstone, 2005; Gaertner & Dovidio, 2000; Pettigrew, 1998)

**Need**: Multilevel models integrating:
- Positive facilitators AND negative inhibitors
- Individual factors (anxiety, empathy, perspective-taking, authoritarianism)
- Situational factors (Allport's conditions, group salience, contact quality)
- Structural factors (institutional support, norms, power relations)
- Temporal dynamics (short-term vs. long-term effects)

**4. Domain Specificity**
- Does learning generalize across attitude domains?
- Can environmental attitude effects transfer to other issues?
- Unknown boundary conditions for generalization

**5. Practical Applications**
- How to implement contact programs effectively?
- What level of contact/exposure is optimal?
- How to scale contact interventions?
- How to sustain effects over time?
