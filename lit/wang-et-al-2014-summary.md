# Perspective-Taking Increases Willingness to Engage in Intergroup Contact

**Authors:** Cynthia S. Wang, Kenneth Tai, Gillian Ku, Adam D. Galinsky  
**Published:** PLoS ONE 2014, Volume 9(1): e85681  
**DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pone.0085681

---

## Core Thesis

Perspective-taking—actively imagining another's viewpoint—increases individuals' willingness to engage in contact with negatively-stereotyped outgroup members. This effect extends to other members of the target's group but not to unrelated stereotyped groups, suggesting perspective-taking is a group-specific social strategy for building bonds.

---

## Theoretical Framework

### The Contact-Prejudice Dilemma

**The Problem:**
- Intergroup contact reduces prejudice and stereotyping
- BUT prejudice itself prevents willingness to engage in contact
- Result: A catch-22 where the solution (contact) is blocked by the problem (prejudice)

**The Question:**
How can we increase willing intergroup contact when people avoid stereotyped outgroups?

### Why Perspective-Taking Should Work

**Galinsky et al.'s Theory:**
Perspective-taking is an approach-oriented strategy geared toward creating and maintaining social bonds.

**Two Mechanisms:**

1. **Self-other merging**: During perspective-taking, cognitive representations of self and other overlap. People see more of themselves in others, making them more likely to approach those individuals.

2. **Reduced prejudice**: Perspective-taking decreases negative evaluations of targets and their groups. Since prejudice creates reluctance to initiate contact, reducing prejudice should increase willingness for contact.

### Alternative Strategies and Their Limitations

**Stereotype Suppression:**
- Intuitively appealing: Just don't think stereotypical thoughts
- Reality: Post-suppression rebound effects increase both prejudicial thoughts AND avoidance behavior
- Macrae et al. (1994): Suppressors sat further away from stereotyped targets than controls

**Structural Changes:**
- Eliminating societal constraints (education, housing differences)
- Reality: Group members still reluctant to engage even when opportunities exist
- Contact when intergroup suspicion is high can worsen prejudice

**Other Effective Strategies:**
- Interdependence (working together toward shared goals)
- Focusing on emotional reactions to discrimination
- Highlighting similarities with outgroup targets

### Group Specificity Hypothesis

Based on prior research (Shih et al., 2009; Vescio et al., 2003):
- Perspective-taking effects tend to be group-specific
- Taking perspective of Asians → more positive toward Asians, NOT toward other groups
- Taking perspective of African Americans → more positive toward African Americans, NOT toward homosexuals

**Prediction:** Perspective-taking will increase contact willingness with the target's group but not generalize to other stereotyped groups.

## Study 1: Physical Proximity as Contact Measure

### Design

**Location:** National University of Singapore  
**N:** 116 undergraduates  
**Target:** "Ah Beng" (local hooligan stereotype)  
**Between-subjects conditions:**
1. Perspective-taking
2. Stereotype suppression
3. Objective focus (control)
4. No instructions (control)

### Pretest Validation (N = 17)

**Ah Beng stereotypes (all significantly above midpoint):**
- Aggressive
- Crude
- Reckless

**Overall favorability:** Significantly negative (below midpoint, t(16) = 6.34, p < .001)

### Procedure

1. **Essay writing**: Participants shown photograph of Ah Beng (young Asian male with spiky hair and tattoos), asked to write about a day in his life
   - **Perspective-taking**: "Take the perspective...imagine a day in the life as if you were that person, looking at the world through his eyes"
   - **Suppression**: "Actively try to avoid thinking about the photographed individual in a stereotypical manner"
   - **Objective**: "Be as objective as possible...Try not to get caught up in imagining what this person has been through or how the person feels"
   - **Control**: "Please compose a brief passage describing a typical day in the life of the individual"

2. **Contact opportunity**: Told they would meet the photographed person

3. **Seating distance measure**: Entered room with 8 seats in a row, helmet on first seat (indicating target's location). Participant chose where to sit (seats 2-8).

### Results

**Main effect:** F(3, 112) = 3.58, p < .02

**Key comparisons:**
- **Perspective-taking vs. Suppression**: 
  - PT sat closer (M = 2.70, SD = .65) vs. Suppression (M = 3.30, SD = .87)
  - t(112) = 3.27, p = .001, d = .78
  
- **Perspective-taking vs. Controls**:
  - PT marginally closer than objective (M = 2.96) and control (M = 3.00)
  - t(112) = 1.83, p = .07, d = .45

- **Suppression vs. Controls**:
  - Suppressors sat further than controls
  - t(112) = 1.97, p = .05, d = .42

- **No difference** between objective and control conditions (t(112) = .20, p = .84)

**Interpretation:** Perspective-taking uniquely increased approach behavior (sitting closer), while suppression uniquely increased avoidance (sitting further).

### Essay Coding (N = 78 available)

**Method:** Coded for stereotypicality using Monteith et al.'s (1998) system. Generated exhaustive stereotype list, parsed essays into thought units, calculated proportion of stereotypical content.

**Results:**
- **Suppressors** wrote LESS stereotypical essays (M = .22) than perspective-takers (M = .47, p = .006, d = 1.06) and controls (M = .37-.52)
- **Perspective-takers** didn't differ from controls (p = .70)

**Implication:** Despite sitting closer, perspective-takers didn't show reduced stereotypicality in essays (contradicts some prior findings—see Study 3 for comparison).

## Study 2: Individual Differences Approach

### Design

**Location:** National University of Singapore  
**N:** 31 undergraduates  
**Target:** Same Ah Beng from Study 1  
**Approach:** Measured individual differences in perspective-taking and empathic tendencies

### Key Measures

**Interpersonal Reactivity Index (Davis, 1980):**
- **Perspective-taking subscale** (7 items, α = .79): "I sometimes try to understand my friends better by imagining how things look from their perspective"
- **Empathic concern subscale** (7 items, α = .69): "I often have tender, concerned feelings for people less fortunate than me"

### Procedure

1. Shown photograph of Ah Beng, wrote essay using neutral control instructions
2. Completed IRI while "waiting" to meet target
3. Experimenter returned, said couldn't find target
4. **DV**: Asked if willing to meet target on another day to exchange life experiences (yes/no)

### Results

**Overall willingness:** 15 participants (48.4%) agreed to meet

**Logistic Regression:**

| Model | Predictors | Effect |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | PT tendencies only | β = 1.46 (.69), p ≤ .05 |
| 2 | PT + Empathy | PT: β = 1.52 (.71), p ≤ .05<br>Empathy: β = -.39 (.74), ns |
| 3 | PT + Empathy + Age + Gender | PT: β = 1.61 (.75), p ≤ .05<br>Others: ns |

**Key Finding:** Perspective-taking tendencies predicted willingness to engage in contact **above and beyond** empathic concern.

**Correlations:**
- PT tendencies with contact willingness: r = .41, p ≤ .05
- Empathy with contact willingness: r = -.03, ns
- PT and empathy tendencies: r = .13, ns

**Interpretation:** Natural variations in perspective-taking (not empathy) predict willingness for intergroup contact, even when all participants received neutral instructions and were exposed to the same stereotype.

## Study 3: Generalization and Boundaries

### Design

**Location:** London Business School (UK)  
**N:** 112 (English native speakers only)  
**Target for manipulation:** Homeless man  
**2×2 Between-subjects design:**
- Essay instructions: Perspective-taking vs. Control
- Contact target: Same group (another homeless man) vs. Different group (man of African descent)

### Pretest Validation (N = 14)

**Homeless stereotypes (all significantly above midpoint):**
- Aggressive
- Dirty
- Disruptive
- Lazy
- Needy
- Sickly

**Overall favorability:** Significantly negative (t(13) = 6.20, p < .001)

### Procedure

1. **Essay writing**: Shown photograph of homeless man (unkempt, lying next to garbage can), wrote about a day in his life
   - Perspective-taking or control instructions (same as Study 1)

2. **Contact measure**: Shown photograph of different person:
   - **Same target group**: Another homeless man
   - **Different target group**: Man of African descent

3. **DV**: Told person agreed to participate in study requiring interaction. Asked:
   - Would you participate with this person? (yes/no)
   - How many tasks would you complete with them? (1-6)
   - Combined into single measure (0-6 tasks)

### Results

**Interaction effect:** F(1, 108) = 3.94, p = .05

**Same Target Group Condition:**
- PT chose more tasks (M = 4.69, SD = 2.31) than controls (M = 3.35, SD = 2.74)
- t(108) = 2.05, p = .04, d = .53

**Different Target Group Condition:**
- No difference between PT (M = 4.13, SD = 2.43) and controls (M = 4.64, SD = 2.22)
- t(108) = .76, p = .45, d = .14

**Pattern:** Perspective-taking increased contact with another homeless person but NOT with a person of African descent.

### Essay Coding (N = 112)

**Method:** Same as Study 1

**Results:**
- **Perspective-takers** wrote LESS stereotypical essays (M = .49, SD = .25) than controls (M = .61, SD = .30)
- t(110) = 2.29, p < .03, d = .44

**Note:** This contradicts Study 1 findings but aligns with Galinsky & Moskowitz (2000).

## Cross-Study Integration

### Summary of Main Effects Across Studies

| Study | Manipulation/Measure | Target | Outcome | Effect Size |
|-------|---------------------|---------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Manipulated PT | Ah Beng | Physical proximity (seating) | d = .78 (vs. suppression)<br>d = .45 (vs. control) |
| 2 | Measured PT tendencies | Ah Beng | Willingness to meet | r = .41 |
| 3 | Manipulated PT | Homeless (same group) | Tasks willing to do | d = .53 |
| 3 | Manipulated PT | African descent (diff group) | Tasks willing to do | d = .14 (ns) |

### Key Patterns

1. **Perspective-taking consistently increases contact willingness** across different operationalizations (physical distance, agreement to meet, number of tasks)

2. **Effects are group-specific**: Benefits extend to target's group but not to other stereotyped groups

3. **Cultural generalizability**: Effects replicated in Eastern (Singapore) and Western (UK) cultures

4. **Individual differences matter**: Natural PT tendencies predict behavior even without manipulation

5. **PT outperforms other strategies**:
   - Better than suppression (which backfires)
   - Better than objective focus
   - Better than neutral control

## Mechanisms and Mediators

### What Mediates PT Effects?

**From Study 2:** Perspective-taking tendencies (not empathy) drive effects

**Theoretical mechanisms:**
1. **Self-other overlap**: Merging of self and other mental representations
2. **Reduced prejudice**: Decreased negative evaluations

**Essay stereotypicality findings:**
- **Study 1**: PT didn't reduce stereotypicality vs. controls (but suppressors showed less)
- **Study 3**: PT reduced stereotypicality vs. controls

**Authors' interpretation:** Inconsistent findings make it difficult to conclude whether essay stereotypicality mediates downstream effects on contact behavior.

### Why Group-Specific?

**Theory:** If PT is geared toward building and maintaining **specific** social bonds, effects should be targeted rather than diffuse.

**Evidence:**
- Study 3: PT → increased contact with target's group, NOT other groups
- Consistent with prior work showing PT reduces bias toward target group but not other groups

## Theoretical Contributions

### 1. Solving the Contact-Prejudice Dilemma

**The Problem:** Prejudice prevents contact, but contact is needed to reduce prejudice

**The Solution:** Perspective-taking simultaneously:
- Reduces prejudicial frame of mind
- Increases willingness for contact
- Creates conditions for positive interaction

**Result:** A "virtuous cycle" where PT → less prejudice + more contact → further prejudice reduction

### 2. Not All Social Strategies Are Equal

**Comparison of strategies:**
- **Suppression**: Backfires, increases avoidance
- **Objective focus**: Ineffective, no better than control
- **Perspective-taking**: Uniquely increases approach

**Parallel to multiculturalism vs. color-blindness:**
- Trying to be objective/color-blind less effective than engaging with multicultural reality
- Trying to suppress less effective than actively taking perspective

### 3. Approach vs. Avoidance Orientations

**Galinsky et al.'s framework:**
- PT = approach-oriented strategy
- Suppression = avoidance-oriented strategy

**Behavioral manifestation:**
- PT → sitting closer, agreeing to meet, volunteering for more tasks
- Suppression → sitting further away

### 4. Group-Specific Bond Formation

**Key insight:** PT isn't a general "reduce all prejudice" tool—it's targeted

**Implications:**
- Need repeated PT with different groups to build broad coalitions
- Can't use PT with one group to fix relations with all groups
- PT is about specific relationship-building, not general tolerance

### 5. Cultural Robustness

**Eastern vs. Western self-construals:**
- Easterners: Interdependent, collectivistic self
- Westerners: Independent, individualistic self

**Finding:** PT works in both Singapore (Eastern) and UK (Western)

**Theoretical note:** Given that self is fundamental to PT (self-other merging), cross-cultural replication is noteworthy.

## Reconciling Contradictory Findings

### Vorauer and Colleagues' Research

**Their findings:** PT can increase evaluative concerns and lead to MORE negative intergroup interactions

**Key differences from current research:**

1. **Statistical pattern**: Vorauer shows interaction with initial prejudice—PT backfires for low-prejudice individuals, not a main negative effect

2. **Procedural differences**:
   - **Vorauer**: 
     - PT manipulation occurs AFTER participants know about upcoming interaction
     - Interaction explicitly focused on outgroup target's experiences
   - **Current research**:
     - PT manipulation BEFORE any awareness of interaction
     - Interaction not explicitly focused on stigmatized individual's experiences

3. **Target groups**:
   - **Vorauer**: Focused on Aboriginal Canadians
   - **Current research**: Hooligans, homeless, African descent—multiple groups with positive PT effects

**Authors' interpretation:** Future research should examine:
- When PT manipulation occurs (before vs. after awareness of interaction)
- Focus of intergroup interaction
- Which stigmatized groups are involved

## Practical Implications

### For Intergroup Interventions

**Two requirements for success:**
1. Decrease prejudicial thoughts
2. Increase intergroup contact

**PT uniquely accomplishes both:**
- Immediately reduces prejudice (established in prior work)
- Increases contact willingness (shown here)

**Additional benefits:**
- Increases behavioral coordination during interactions
- Improves satisfaction with interactions
- Promotes approach behaviors

### The Virtuous Cycle

**Stage 1:** PT manipulation
- ↓ Prejudicial thoughts
- ↑ Willingness for contact

**Stage 2:** Positive contact occurs
- ↓ Prejudice further (contact theory)
- Behavioral coordination improves (PT enhances interactions)

**Stage 3:** Long-lasting positive bonds
- Reinforces reduced prejudice
- Maintains social connections

### Implementation Considerations

**When to use PT:**
- Before intergroup contact occurs
- When intergroup suspicion isn't extreme
- When goal is building specific relationships

**When PT may not work:**
- High intergroup suspicion/enmity already present
- Need for immediate broad coalition across multiple groups
- When interaction explicitly focuses on stereotype/stigma

**Targeting:**
- Need group-specific PT interventions
- Taking perspective of homeless person helps with homeless people, not other groups
- Can't use single PT intervention to improve all intergroup relations

## Methodological Strengths

1. **Multiple operationalizations of contact**:
   - Physical proximity (seating distance)
   - Agreement to future interaction
   - Number of tasks willing to complete together

2. **Both experimental and correlational evidence**:
   - Manipulated PT (Studies 1, 3)
   - Measured PT tendencies (Study 2)

3. **Comprehensive comparison**:
   - PT vs. suppression vs. objective focus vs. control
   - Allows assessment of relative effectiveness

4. **Boundary testing**:
   - Same vs. different target groups
   - Tests generalization limits

5. **Cross-cultural replication**:
   - Singapore (Eastern) and UK (Western)
   - Multiple target groups (hooligans, homeless, African descent)

6. **Behavioral measures**:
   - Actual seating choices
   - Real commitments to future interaction
   - Not just self-reported attitudes

7. **Control for empathy**:
   - Study 2 shows PT effects beyond empathic concern
   - Distinguishes related but distinct constructs

## Limitations and Unanswered Questions

### Inconsistent Findings

**Essay stereotypicality:**
- Study 1: PT didn't reduce stereotypicality vs. controls
- Study 3: PT did reduce stereotypicality vs. controls
- Unclear whether stereotypicality mediates contact behavior

**Authors' caution:** Given reduced sample (N=78) in Study 1 and inconsistency, can't draw firm conclusions about mechanism.

### Boundary Conditions

**Questions remaining:**
- How extreme can prejudice be before PT backfires?
- When does knowledge of upcoming interaction change PT effects?
- Which target groups might show different patterns?
- How long do effects last?

### Target Selection

**All targets were visibly identifiable:**
- Ah Beng: Spiky hair, tattoos
- Homeless: Unkempt appearance
- African descent: Race

**Question:** Would PT work for concealable stigmas (e.g., mental illness, sexual orientation when not visible)?

### Interaction Quality

**Not measured:**
- What happens during actual interactions?
- Do PT-induced interactions go better than control interactions?
- Does increased contact willingness translate to better interaction quality?
